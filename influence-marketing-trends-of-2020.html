Influence Marketing Trends of 2020

In this article you’ll read second most important digital marketing trend of 2020 – “Influence Marketing”. Here are the latest trends of influence marketing:

7. Micro-Influencers Will Be More Sought After

According to InfluencerDB, influencer engagement rates are declining and have even reached a historically low level (barely 1.9% for sponsored posts in Q1 of 2019).

The reason? - There are far too many sponsored posts on Instagram.

The solution? - Collaborate with more influencers (quantity), but those with a higher engagement rate. In other words, brands will have to turn to micro-influencers.

Anyway, influencers are already very busy and solicited by dozens of brands. If they are solicited, they necessarily cost more (law of supply and demand). In addition, they are more skilled in negotiation or are accompanied by agencies. Micro-influencers, by contrast, have small targeted communities.

What size, will you tell me? - It depends.

You can say that you are considered a "micro-influencer" if you have a community that is between 1000 and 100,000 people. This is the slice that comes back most often. It all depends on the industry. For example, there are hundreds of fashion bloggers on Instagram whose number of subscribers is in the tens of thousands. They are considered micro-influencers. In digital marketing, however, few influencers have more than 50,000 subscribers. And if there is one, we will obviously not consider him a micro-influencer.

What is the big difference from the "big" influencers?

There are several. Micro-influencers have more engaged communities, i.e. they reap a higher interaction rate for each post. Typically, micro-influencers have interaction rates per post that can go up to 20%! (Yes, you read it right.) Such a rate of interaction is unthinkable for an influencer.

According to InfluencerDB, the engagement rate of Instagram influencers with at least 10,000 followers remains stable at around 3.6%. On the other hand, influencers with 5,000 to 10,000 subscribers have a commitment rate of 6.3% and those with between 1,000 and 5,000 subscribers have an even higher commitment rate of 8.8%. Because micro-influencers have a smaller community and are less sought after by brands, they cost less!

Icing on the cake, they are closer to their subscribers, which means that when they promote a product, there is a greater chance that they will believe it. Unlike celebrities to millions of followers trying to sell a product. We all know that none of this is true. According to the agency HelloSociety, “campaigns that appeal to micro-influencers have a 60% higher engagement rate and push 6.7 times more followers towards buying behavior than large prescribers, making advertiser strategies much more profitable.”

You understand why it is interesting to develop relationships with micro-influencers. Maybe like me you did not know the brand Rosefield, the one that was highlighted in the post I showed you.

But do you know Daniel Wellington?

Probably! This brand also appeals to micro-influencers. This account has just 2250 subscribers and the interaction rate is very high too. In 2020, it can be expected that micro-influencers will be more sought after by brands.

8. Will Influencers Post More or Less?

While doing my digital watch, I came across an interesting article that asks the following question:

"Assuming Instagram will end up hiding likes in 2020, will influencers start publishing more?”

The end of likes on Instagram is approaching. The test is also being deployed in France since the end of 2019. Later and Fohr showed in a new study on influence marketing that influencers post less in their feed. They published an average of 3.3 times a week in 2016. They already publish only 2.7 times in 2019.

Today, Instagram users have this tendency to publish only their "best" content in the news feed in order to maintain a high engagement rate in all their posts. According to the same study, later and Fohr estimate that influencers will no longer publish "only" 2 times a week, on average, in their feed. But by removing the likes, Instagram assumes that users will be more creative and publish what they want without worrying about how many likes they get on a post. If so, it can be assumed that hiding likes will prompt Instagram users (including influencers) to post more.

Anyway, these are just thoughts that I find interesting to share with you. Instagram has yet to roll out the end of the likes counter on Instagram all over the world in 2020. And it seems likely if we rely on the latest news.

It can already be concluded, following the influence marketing report published by later and Fohr, that influencers who will take the time to post at least 3 times a week will have an advantage over their competitors.
